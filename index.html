Stan culture or submission to the death of individuality?

By The Fellowship · Reading time: 8 minutes

Let's start with the Oxford definition of the word. It defines a "stan" as "an overzealous or obsessive fan of a particular celebrity." In practice, that's bullshit. What many people pledge themselves under "Stan culture" is, from my unsympathetic perspective, groups of mindless individuals who protect their catalyzed image of whoever or whatever they call themselves stanning.

Now, I'm not saying all people that stan are parasocial freaks that treat these individuals as besties. But what I am saying is the inclusion and acceptance of these people under the same umbrella you deem yourself bleeds the same actions and intent into these spaces. Harboring this almost primal and toxic defensive environment where, for the lack of a better term, cult-like behaviors emerge.

“No, bitch, you cannot think. You're worthless human matter if you don't follow our metrics.” — The Hive Mind Translation

The Barbs: A case study in cognitive dissonance

Let's talk about the most glaring example in pop culture right now: the Barbs. I'm not gonna go deep into every detail because even acknowledging this will get me slandered on X by people who've never had an original thought in their lives. But here are facts—not opinions, not hate speech, facts:

  • Nicki Minaj is married to a registered sex offender.
  • Nicki Minaj is now a devoted MAGA supporter and Trump super stan.
  • Nicki Minaj has publicly aligned with homophobic rhetoric and figures.

Now, let's use logic for a second—not politics, not morality, just straight logic. If you don't understand why it's logically broken for a demographic to ride hard for someone whose actions and alliances actively work against their existence—then you shouldn't be reading my writing.

The real human need nobody wants to admit

People don't join stan cultures because they're stupid or evil. They join because they're lonely. In 2026, genuine human connection is harder to come by than ever. We don't know our neighbors. We don't have third spaces. So we find connection the only way that's available: online, in communities built around shared consumption.

The problem is that these communities, as currently structured, demand a price for entry: your individuality.

— The Fellowship
