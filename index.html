Stan culture
or submission to the death of individuality?

By The Fellowship • 8 Min Read

Let's start with the Oxford definition of the word. It defines a "stan" as "an overzealous or obsessive fan of a particular celebrity." In practice, that's bullshit. What many people pledge themselves under "Stan culture" is, from my unsympathetic perspective, groups of mindless individuals who protect their catalyzed image of whoever or whatever they call themselves stanning.

Now, I'm not saying all people that stan are parasocial freaks that treat these individuals as besties. But what I am saying is the inclusion and acceptance of these people under the same umbrella you deem yourself bleeds the same actions and intent into these spaces.

Case Study: Dissonance

Let's talk about the most glaring example in pop culture right now: the Barbs. I'm not gonna go deep into every detail because even acknowledging this will get me slandered on X by people who've never had an original thought in their lives.

Nicki Minaj is married to a registered sex offender. Nicki Minaj is now a devoted MAGA supporter. Nicki Minaj has publicly aligned with homophobic rhetoric. These are facts—not opinions. If you don't understand why it's logically broken for a demographic to ride hard for someone whose actions work against their existence—then you shouldn't be reading my writing.

The Barbs protest for this woman like they personally emerged from her body. They treat streams and chart positions as more important than actual human rights.

“The same people who defend this will do it for way less. That’s the problem.”

The Web

We are raising—have raised—generations of people who don't know how to be a person outside of who they stan, what music they listen to, who they follow, what they post, what algorithm they're trapped in. We've outsourced identity to content.

People don't join stan cultures because they're stupid. They join because they're lonely. In 2026, genuine human connection is harder to come by than ever. We don't have third spaces. So we find connection the only way that's available: online, in communities built around shared consumption.
